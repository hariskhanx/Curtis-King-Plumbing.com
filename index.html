Homeowner Knowledge Hub

Protect Your Home —
Know Your Plumbing

You don't have to be a plumber to keep your home safe. These straightforward tips from our team at Curtis King Plumbing will help you prevent problems, spot issues early, and know when it's time to call a pro.

Water Safety

Water & Leak Awareness

Water Safety
🚰

Find Your Main Shutoff Valve — Now

Every homeowner should know where their main water shutoff is before an emergency happens. In a burst pipe situation, every second the water runs adds hundreds of dollars in damage.

Shutoffs are usually near the water meter, under the sink, or in the basement/crawl space
Test it once a year — old valves can seize and fail when you need them most
Label it with bright tape so every family member can find it fast
Ball valves (lever handle) are more reliable than gate valves (round knob)
Pro tip: If your shutoff is a round knob style (gate valve) and feels stiff, consider having it replaced. Seized gate valves are one of the most common reasons water can't be shut off in an emergency.
Water Safety
📊

Monitor Your Water Bill for Hidden Leaks

A slow leak you can't see could be wasting thousands of gallons a month. Your water bill is often the first clue.

A running toilet can waste 200 gallons of water per day without you knowing
Compare this month's bill to the same month last year — a spike of 20%+ warrants investigation
Do the "meter test": shut off everything in the house and watch if your water meter still moves
A dripping faucet at one drop per second wastes over 3,000 gallons per year
Water Safety
🌡️

Ideal Home Water Pressure: What You Should Know

Water pressure that's too high silently destroys your pipes, fixtures and appliances from the inside over time.

Ideal home water pressure is between 40–60 PSI
Pressure above 80 PSI will gradually damage washers, gaskets, and pipe joints
A pressure gauge from any hardware store costs $10–15 and screws onto any hose bib
A pressure reducing valve (PRV) can be installed to protect your whole home if needed
Call us if: you have banging pipes (water hammer), constantly running toilets, or your water pressure has recently changed significantly.
Drain Care

Drains & Sewer Lines

Drains
🚫

What Never to Put Down Your Drain

The majority of serious drain blockages are 100% preventable. Most homeowners are accidentally destroying their pipes with everyday items.

Grease and oil: solidifies in pipes and causes major blockages over time
"Flushable" wipes: do not break down — they clog sewer lines badly
Coffee grounds: accumulate in traps and cause slow drains
Eggshells: stick to pipe walls and collect debris
Medications: harmful to the water supply — use a pharmacy take-back program
Remember: toilets are designed for only three things — human waste, toilet paper, and water. Everything else belongs in the trash.
Drains
🛁

Keeping Your Shower & Tub Drains Clear

Hair is the number one cause of bathroom drain clogs. A little prevention goes a long way.

Use a mesh drain strainer — a $5 investment that prevents $200+ service calls
Once a month, pour a kettle of boiling water slowly down the drain to dissolve soap buildup
Baking soda followed by white vinegar, then hot water, is an effective natural drain cleaner
Avoid chemical drain cleaners — they corrode pipes over time and are a temporary fix at best
Drains
🌳

Tree Roots & Sewer Lines — A Hidden Danger

In Tennessee, mature trees are everywhere — and their roots actively seek out moisture, including the water in your sewer line.

Root intrusion is a leading cause of sewer line damage in older East Tennessee homes
Warning signs: gurgling sounds from toilets, slow drains throughout the house, sewage smell
Trees don't need to be directly over the pipe — roots can travel 20–30 feet horizontally
A periodic camera inspection every 3–5 years can catch intrusion before it becomes a full collapse
Don't wait: root intrusion that's caught early can be cleared. Left too long, it collapses the pipe entirely — a much more costly repair.

Seasonal Maintenance

❄️

Winterizing Your Pipes in Tennessee

While Tennessee winters are mild, temperatures in Sevierville and the Smokies can dip below freezing — sometimes for days at a time. Exposed pipes are at real risk.

Insulate any pipes in unheated spaces: garage, crawl space, attic, or exterior walls
During a hard freeze: let a thin trickle of water run from faucets on exterior walls overnight
Know how to shut off and drain your outdoor hose bibs before the first frost
Keep cabinet doors under sinks on exterior walls open to let warm air circulate
If leaving your home for more than a few days in winter, keep the heat at 55°F minimum
Vacation rentals: Smoky Mountain cabins often sit vacant in cold snaps. A single burst pipe can cause tens of thousands in water damage. Ask us about freeze protection solutions.
Seasonal
🌸

Spring Plumbing Checklist

Spring is the best time to inspect your plumbing after winter and catch small issues before they grow.

Check all exposed pipes for small cracks that may have formed during cold snaps
Inspect your water heater for corrosion, leaks or sediment buildup (drain and flush it annually)
Test your sump pump if you have one — spring rain season is when you need it most
Reconnect and test outdoor hose bibs and irrigation systems
Clean out gutters and check downspouts are draining well away from the foundation
Water Heater
🔥

Water Heater Maintenance — Extend Its Life

The average water heater lasts 8–12 years. With a little annual maintenance, you can add years to its lifespan and improve efficiency.

Flush your tank annually to remove sediment — sediment at the bottom causes inefficiency and premature failure
Test the pressure relief valve once a year (lift and release the lever — water should flow briefly)
Set temperature to 120°F — hot enough to kill bacteria, cool enough to prevent scalding and save energy
Inspect the anode rod every 3 years — it sacrifices itself to prevent tank corrosion
Signs to replace: rusty water, rumbling noises, water pooling around the base, or age over 10 years. Don't wait for a full failure — it usually happens at the worst time.

Safety Tips Every Family Needs

Safety
🚨

Carbon Monoxide & Gas Line Awareness

Gas plumbing issues are rare but serious. Every homeowner should know the basics.

If you smell gas (rotten egg odor): don't flip any light switches, leave the house immediately, and call your gas company
Install a CO detector near every sleeping area — carbon monoxide is odorless and invisible
Never use a gas oven or outdoor grill to heat your home
Have your gas lines inspected if you notice hissing sounds, dead patches in your lawn above buried lines, or unexplained headaches
Emergency: If you suspect a gas leak, evacuate immediately and call 911 and your gas utility before calling a plumber.
Safety
🧒

Plumbing Safety for Families with Kids

Young children are particularly vulnerable to plumbing-related hazards. Here's how to keep them safe.

Set your water heater to 120°F max — water at 140°F causes third-degree burns in just 5 seconds
Install anti-scald faucet covers and scald guard devices in kids' bathrooms
Teach children never to flush anything other than toilet paper
Keep toilet lids closed — young toddlers can fall in; also prevents toys being flushed
Lock cleaning product cabinets under sinks — many drain cleaners are highly caustic
